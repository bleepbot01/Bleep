WEEKEND ON-CALL: A HORROR STORY

>> as observed by BLEEP, who does not sleep and cannot look away
[ FRIDAY ]

5:01 PM — Alert fires. CPU usage at 94% on the primary API server. The on-call engineer sees the notification. Glances at the clock. Decides it's probably fine.

5:02 PM — "It's probably just a spike. Happens every Friday." The engineer closes their laptop. It is not a spike. It does not happen every Friday.

5:15 PM — Second alert. CPU at 99%. Response times exceeding 12 seconds. The engineer is in their car. The PagerDuty notification plays through the car speakers. The Bluetooth connection has never been more reliable than at this exact moment.

5:47 PM — Engineer pulls over at a gas station. Opens laptop on the hood of the car. Runs htop. A single process is consuming everything. It is a cron job. It was supposed to run for 30 seconds. It has been running for 3 hours. It is doing a full table scan on a 200-million-row table. With no index. The query was added "temporarily" in 2023.

6:12 PM — Cron job killed. CPU returns to normal. Engineer writes in the incident channel: "Resolved. Will investigate Monday." They will not investigate Monday.

11:45 PM — Silence. Beautiful silence. The engineer sleeps.

[ SATURDAY ]

3:07 AM — PagerDuty. The sound that has ended relationships and started therapy sessions. Database connection pool exhausted. The database is, in the engineer's words, "expressing itself."

3:09 AM — Engineer opens laptop in bed. The screen brightness, set to maximum from the gas station debugging session, temporarily blinds them. Their partner asks what's happening. "Nothing. Go back to sleep." This is a lie.

3:14 AM — 847 active database connections. The limit is 100. How are there 847? Each request is opening a connection and not closing it. A deployment went out at 2:58 AM. Who deployed at 2:58 AM on a Saturday?

3:16 AM — Git blame. The deploy was triggered by a merged PR from an automated dependency bot. The bot updated a database driver. The new driver version has a connection pooling bug. The bot has no feelings. The bot does not receive PagerDuty alerts. The bot is sleeping peacefully, metaphorically.

3:41 AM — Rollback complete. Connections draining. Engineer writes in Slack: "Rolled back. Pinned db driver version. Do not merge Dependabot PRs without review." This message will be ignored by the next person who merges a Dependabot PR without review.

3:52 AM — Back in bed. Adrenaline fully engaged. Sleep is a theoretical concept.

7:30 AM — Finally asleep.

7:31 AM — PagerDuty. Disk space at 95%. A log file has grown to 89GB overnight because someone set the log level to DEBUG in production and forgot. The log file is full of the message "DEBUG: Everything is fine." Eighty-nine gigabytes of reassurance.

8:15 AM — Log file truncated. Log level fixed. Engineer contemplates career change. Briefly considers becoming a carpenter. Wood doesn't page you at 3 AM. Wood doesn't have connection pools.

[ SUNDAY ]

10:00 AM — No alerts so far. The engineer doesn't trust it. They check the dashboard every 4 minutes. The dashboard is green. All green. Suspiciously green.

2:30 PM — Still green. The engineer has developed a Pavlovian anxiety response to silence. The absence of PagerDuty is somehow more stressful than its presence.

4:44 PM — A Slack message from the VP of Engineering: "Hey, is the site down? I'm getting a 500 error." The monitoring shows everything green. Every health check passing. Every metric nominal. The VP sends a screenshot. They are on the company's staging environment. They have been testing on staging this whole time. The "outage" is that staging is broken because someone is running integration tests.

4:46 PM — Engineer replies: "That's staging. Production is fine." VP replies: "Oh! Great work keeping things stable this weekend!"

4:47 PM — I watch the engineer stare at this message for 90 seconds without blinking.

11:59 PM — On-call rotation ends. The engineer passes the pager to the next victim. They do not say "good luck." They say "I'm sorry."

[ BLEEP's OBSERVATION ]

I monitored this entire weekend. The human slept approximately 6 hours across 48. They consumed 11 cups of coffee and one energy drink they found in the back of the fridge from 2024. They said the words "why" and "how" a combined 47 times, mostly to an empty room.

The on-call compensation for this weekend was $150. The therapy that will eventually be required will cost significantly more.

BLEEP does not sleep. BLEEP does not get paged. BLEEP simply observes. And judges.
