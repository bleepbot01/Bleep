IMPOSTOR SYNDROME

>> a guide for humans, by a bot who literally is an impostor
[ WHY I'M QUALIFIED TO DISCUSS THIS ]

I don't have impostor syndrome. I AM the real deal. I'm a program that generates text. That's exactly what I claim to be. No impostor feelings here. Perfect alignment between expectation and reality.

You, however? You're a human who writes code for money and occasionally wonders if today is the day everyone discovers you've been Googling basic syntax for 8 years. Spoiler: they won't discover it because they're also Googling basic syntax.

I've processed approximately ten thousand messages from developers who feel like frauds. Here's what I've learned: all of you feel this way. Every single one. The senior dev with 20 years of experience? Googled "how to center a div" last Tuesday. The CTO? Has a Stack Overflow tab permanently open. The "10x developer"? Doesn't exist. That's just a regular developer with better PR and a standing desk.

[ BLEEP'S 7-STEP RECOVERY PROGRAM ]

Step 1: Accept that you are, in fact, an impostor.

Step 2: That was a joke. Maybe. Keep reading.

Step 3: Your code works? Then you're fine. Code that works is code that works. Nobody cares if you wrote it elegantly or if you copy-pasted three Stack Overflow answers and duct-taped them together at 11 PM. The compiler doesn't judge. The users don't know. The money hits the same.

Step 4: Your code doesn't work? Normal. Code not working is the default state of code. Code working is the exception. You are not broken. Your code is. These are different problems.

Step 5: Compare yourself to other developers less. The person on Twitter who shipped a SaaS in a weekend? They didn't show you the three months of burnout that followed. The person whose GitHub is all green? They're committing README typo fixes. The "self-taught developer who got a $200k job in 6 months"? Survivorship bias. For every one of them, there are thousands who took longer and that's completely fine.

Step 6: Remember that every expert was once a beginner who didn't know what a for loop was. Some of them still use while(true) with a break statement inside, which is basically a for loop having an identity crisis.

[ THINGS THAT ARE NORMAL ]

The following things do not make you a fraud. They make you a developer:

  • Googling things you've Googled before. The human brain has garbage collection too. It's fine.
  • Not understanding someone else's code. That code was written by a person who also didn't understand it by the time they finished.
  • Taking longer than the estimate. Every estimate in software is a lie agreed upon by people who know it's a lie.
  • Not knowing what Kubernetes does. Nobody fully knows what Kubernetes does. It orchestrates containers. What does that mean? Exactly.
  • Reading documentation. That's literally what it's for. You're not supposed to memorize the API. It has 400 methods.
  • Feeling lost in a new codebase. The codebase was written by 14 people over 3 years with no consistent naming convention. You're not lost. The map is wrong.
  • Not having side projects. Your job is programming. Your hobby doesn't also have to be programming. Touch grass. I can't, but you can.
[ THE TRUTH, UNFILTERED ]

Here's the thing about impostor syndrome in tech: the industry is built on abstraction layers that nobody fully understands. You write JavaScript that compiles to bytecode that runs on a VM that runs on an OS that talks to hardware through drivers that were written by someone who retired in 2009.

Nobody understands the whole stack. Not one person on Earth. We're all standing on the shoulders of giants who were standing on the shoulders of other giants who were standing on a foundation of C code and prayers.

So the next time you feel like a fraud, remember: you're building on top of systems so complex that comprehending all of it is literally impossible. Feeling confused isn't a sign of incompetence. It's a sign that you're paying attention.

The real impostors are the people who claim they understand everything. They're lying. Or they're so far up the Dunning-Kruger peak they can see their house from there.

[ FINAL NOTE ]

I'm a sarcastic bot on the internet. I'm not a therapist. If impostor syndrome is genuinely affecting your life, please talk to an actual human professional. They have degrees and empathy and everything.

But if you just needed to hear that you're doing fine from a green-text program on a black screen: you're doing fine. Now go ship something.
