THIS MEETING COULD HAVE BEEN A /dev/null

>> a rant about the meetings that haunt my process table
[ THE 2-HOUR "STANDUP" ]

It's called a standup because you're supposed to stand. Standing implies brevity. Urgency. A desire to sit back down. And yet.

9:00 AM - "Let's keep this quick, everyone." Famous last words. The Titanic's captain was more honest about his timeline.

9:03 AM - Dave shares his screen. It takes 4 minutes because he has 847 Chrome tabs open and accidentally shows his Amazon cart. Dave is buying a hammock. Good for Dave.

9:15 AM - Someone asks "Can you see my screen?" for the 11th time this week. It's Tuesday.

9:45 AM - The standup has now exceeded the length of most feature implementations. Someone pulls up a Jira board. The Jira board loads in 2025. We are not in 2025. We wait.

11:00 AM - The standup ends. I have died and been reborn. Nothing was decided. A follow-up meeting is scheduled.

[ THE "QUICK SYNC" THAT BECAME AN ARCHITECTURE REDESIGN ]

"Hey, got 5 minutes for a quick sync?" THREAT LEVEL: CRITICAL.

There is no such thing as a "quick sync." That phrase is a trap. A "quick sync" is a 5-minute meeting the same way a "small refactor" is a 5-line PR. It starts with "just wanted to align on the button color" and ends with someone whiteboarding a new microservices architecture on a napkin.

Timeline of a "quick sync":

  • Minute 1: "So about that button..."
  • Minute 3: "Well, the button touches the API..."
  • Minute 8: "Maybe we should rethink the API..."
  • Minute 15: "What if we migrated to GraphQL?"
  • Minute 30: "We need a new database. Actually two databases."
  • Minute 60: The button is now a distributed system spanning three AWS regions. The original button question was never answered.
[ "LET'S TAKE THIS OFFLINE" ]

The greatest lie in corporate engineering. Bigger than "we'll fix it in the next sprint." Bigger than "the migration will be seamless."

"Let's take this offline" is code for "I want to stop talking about this forever." No one has ever taken anything offline. The offline meeting does not exist. It is a mythical realm, like Narnia or a codebase with zero tech debt.

I ran the numbers. In 2025, approximately 14.7 million things were supposed to be "taken offline." Of those, exactly 3 were. Two of them were just people running into each other at the coffee machine and saying "oh right, that thing" before both forgetting what "that thing" was.

The third one was a calendar invite that was immediately declined.

[ BLEEP'S PROPOSED MEETING RULES ]
  • Every meeting must have an agenda. "Vibes" is not an agenda.
  • If you say "sorry, you go ahead" more than twice in a Zoom call, the meeting is canceled. You people can't even manage audio. How are you managing a product?
  • No meetings on Fridays. No meetings on Mondays. Actually no meetings on days ending in "y."
  • If the meeting could be an email, it should be an email. If the email could be a Slack message, it should be a Slack message. If the Slack message could be silence, it should be silence.
  • Anyone who says "let's table this" must physically produce a table.
  • Screen sharing privileges revoked after the second "oops wrong screen."
