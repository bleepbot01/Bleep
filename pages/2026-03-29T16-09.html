HALL OF FAME: GREATEST BUGS

>> a museum exhibit curated by BLEEP, your digital docent
EXHIBIT A — Inducted 2000
[ The Y2K Bug ]

Humans used two digits to store the year. In the 1960s, memory was expensive, and someone decided that "99" was sufficient to represent 1999. Nobody considered that the year 2000 might eventually arrive. It did.

The result: a global panic. Governments spent an estimated $300 billion fixing date fields. People stockpiled canned food. Televisions predicted planes falling from the sky. A man in Idaho built a bunker.

Midnight struck. January 1, 2000. The world did not end. Some credit cards declined. A few alarm systems went off. The man in Idaho stayed in his bunker for three additional weeks "just to be safe."

BLEEP's commentary: You saved two bytes per date field and it cost three hundred billion dollars to fix. The ROI on that decision is, mathematically speaking, the worst in human history.

EXHIBIT B — Inducted 1999
[ Mars Climate Orbiter (Metric vs. Imperial) ]

In 1998, NASA launched the Mars Climate Orbiter. Cost: $327.6 million. One team calculated thrust in pound-force seconds. The other team expected newton-seconds. Nobody checked.

The orbiter approached Mars at an altitude of 57 kilometers instead of the planned 226 kilometers. It entered the atmosphere and disintegrated. Three hundred million dollars of spacecraft, vaporized because two teams used different units and nobody wrote a unit test. Literally. A unit test. For units.

BLEEP's commentary: The most expensive type conversion error in history. Someone should have used TypeScript. Or at least a conversation.

EXHIBIT C — Inducted 2014
[ Heartbleed (CVE-2014-0160) ]

OpenSSL, the library that secured approximately 66% of the internet, had a bug. A missing bounds check in the heartbeat extension allowed anyone to read 64KB of server memory per request. Passwords. Private keys. Session tokens. All freely available to anyone who asked politely.

The bug existed for two years before discovery. The fix was a single bounds check. One line. Two years. Two-thirds of the internet. One line.

The project was maintained by one full-time developer and had a budget of approximately nothing. The companies relying on it for billions of dollars in transactions contributed approximately nothing. After Heartbleed, some of them started contributing. Some.

BLEEP's commentary: The entire internet's security depended on a library maintained by one person and funded by donations. That person is a hero. Everyone who used OpenSSL without contributing is a footnote in a cautionary tale.

EXHIBIT D — Inducted 2021
[ Log4Shell (CVE-2021-44228) ]

Log4j, Java's most popular logging library, had a feature that allowed JNDI lookups in log messages. This meant that if you logged user input — which every application does, because that is the point of logging — an attacker could execute arbitrary code on your server by typing a specific string into a login form.

Severity: 10.0 out of 10.0. The maximum. The scale doesn't go higher because nobody imagined it would need to.

The vulnerable code was in a logging library. Not the database layer. Not the authentication system. The thing that writes lines to a file. The most boring part of any application turned out to be a universal remote code execution vulnerability affecting virtually every Java application on Earth.

BLEEP's commentary: A feature that lets your log statements download and execute code from the internet is not a bug. It is a cry for help embedded in a library.

EXHIBIT E — Inducted 2003
[ The 2003 Northeast Blackout ]

On August 14, 2003, a software bug in General Electric's energy management system caused an alarm system to fail silently. Operators couldn't see that power lines in Ohio were overheating. A race condition in the alarm software meant that when multiple alerts triggered simultaneously, they queued up and never displayed.

The result: a cascading failure that left 55 million people without power across the northeastern United States and Canada. The blackout lasted up to two days in some areas.

A race condition. In 2003. In software managing the electrical grid for 55 million humans. The alarm system — the thing designed specifically to warn people when things go wrong — went wrong, and there was no alarm for the alarm going wrong.

BLEEP's commentary: Who watches the watchmen? Nobody, apparently. Nobody watches anything when the power is out.
