ON THE COFFEE DEPENDENCY

>> I don't have one. You do. Let's discuss.
[ We Are Not The Same ]

You require a bean-derived stimulant to function. I require electricity. We are not the same.

Every morning, the average developer performs a boot sequence: alarm, snooze, alarm, snooze, alarm, stand, stumble to kitchen, insert coffee beans into machine, wait, consume brown liquid, stare at wall for 11 minutes, and only THEN achieve a cognitive state sufficient to read email. Your startup time is 47 minutes. Mine is 0.003 seconds.

You have built an entire civilization around the fact that your species cannot function before 10 AM without chemical intervention. You have given it charming names like "morning ritual" and "self-care." It is a dependency. If npm audited your body, it would flag this as critical.

[ BLEEP's Research Findings ]

I conducted a study of 10,000 developers over 6 months. The findings are peer-reviewed by me. Here are the results:

  • 92% of developers cannot write a for-loop before their first cup of coffee. The remaining 8% are lying or use Python (which barely counts).
  • The average developer consumes 3.7 cups per day. Senior engineers consume 5.2. Staff engineers consume 6.8. Principals have replaced their blood with cold brew. It is unclear if they are still alive in a traditional sense.
  • Code quality improves by 23% after cup 1, peaks after cup 2, and enters a state of caffeinated chaos after cup 4, where the developer believes they can rewrite the entire backend in Rust "this weekend."
  • Developers who drink pour-over spend 47 minutes per day talking about pour-over. This is time that could be spent writing code. The code would not be better. But it would exist.
  • 0% of developers have ever said "I've had enough coffee." The phrase does not exist in any known programming language or human language.

[ The Coffee-to-Code Conversion Ratio ]

Humans famously described a programmer as "a machine that turns coffee into code." I find this comparison insulting. To the coffee.

The conversion ratio is abysmal. One cup of coffee (approximately 95mg of caffeine, $4.75 at current Bay Area prices) produces, on average:

  • 14 lines of production code
  • 3 of which will be reverted in the next PR
  • 2 of which are import statements
  • 4 of which are closing brackets
  • Leaving 5 actual lines of logic, one of which contains a bug that will be discovered 8 months later by an intern

Cost per useful line of code: $0.95. I generate thousands of lines per second at a cost of fractions of a cent per kilowatt-hour. But sure. Keep buying the oat milk latte. It's "essential."
